Detailed Grammar Points

1. Object Particle: Noun + 을/를

This particle attaches to a noun to mark it as the **object** of a verb. It answers the question "what?" or "whom?" in relation to the verb. For example, in "I eat an apple," "apple" is the object.

Rule:

  • After a noun ending in a **consonant** (받침 있음), use 을.
  • After a noun ending in a **vowel** (받침 없음), use 를.

Examples:

저는 사과를 사요. (I buy an apple.) - '사과' ends in a vowel.

저는 빵을 먹어요. (I eat bread.) - '빵' ends in a consonant.

마이클이 책을 읽어요. (Michael reads a book.) - '책' ends in a consonant.

Tip: Casual Speech

In casual conversation, the object particle 을/를 is often omitted. For example, you might hear "사과 사요" instead of "사과를 사요". However, it's best to practice using it correctly!

2. Polite Request: Verb + (으)세요

This ending is attached to a verb stem to make a polite request or command. It's the equivalent of saying "Please do..." and is extremely common in daily life, especially in stores and restaurants.

Rule:

  • After a verb stem ending in a **consonant**, use 으세요. (e.g., 읽다 → 읽 + 으세요 = 읽으세요)
  • After a verb stem ending in a **vowel**, use 세요. (e.g., 가다 → 가 + 세요 = 가세요)

Examples:

오렌지 주세요. (Please give me an orange.) - From 주다 (to give). The most common shopping phrase.

책을 읽으세요. (Please read the book.) - '읽' ends in a consonant.

여기에 앉으세요. (Please sit here.) - '앉' ends in a consonant.

Sino-Korean Numbers (for Prices)

When talking about money, prices, and phone numbers, Koreans use Sino-Korean numbers (derived from Chinese). The basic unit for large numbers is 만 (10,000).
