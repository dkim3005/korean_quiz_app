Detailed Grammar Points

1. Polite Request: Noun + 주세요

'주세요' is a versatile and essential phrase. It's the polite way to ask for something. You can use it for items, services, or even abstract things. It's a combination of the verb '주다' (to give) and the polite ending '-세요'.

Examples:

물 주세요. (Please give me water.)

사과 한 개 주세요. (Please give me one apple.)

영수증 주세요. (Please give me the receipt.)

2. Connecting Nouns: Noun + 하고 (and)

This particle is used to list nouns, meaning 'and' or 'with'. It's very common in spoken Korean because it's easy to use—you just attach it to the noun regardless of whether it ends in a vowel or consonant.

Examples:

빵하고 우유하고 사과를 샀어요. (I bought bread, milk, and an apple.)

가게에서 사과하고 물을 주세요. (At the store, please give me an apple and water.)

저는 친구하고 학교에 갑니다. (I go to school with my friend.) - Here, it means 'with'.

3. Counting Items: Native Korean Numbers

Korean has two number systems. For counting general items, age, and hours, you use the **Native Korean** system. The first four numbers change their form when followed by a counting word (a "counter").

Rule: Number + Counter (e.g., 개)

  • 1: 하나 → 한 개
  • 2: 둘 → 두 개
  • 3: 셋 → 세 개
  • 4: 넷 → 네 개
  • 5: 다섯 → 다섯 개 (No change from 5 onwards)

Examples:

A: 사과, 몇 개 드릴까요? (How many apples shall I give you?)

B: 세 개 주세요. (Please give me three.)

빵 한 개하고 우유 두 개 주세요. (Please give me one bread and two milks.)

Important Distinction!

Use **Native Korean** numbers for counting items (사과 한 개). Use **Sino-Korean** numbers (일, 이, 삼...) for prices (천 원 - 1,000 won).
