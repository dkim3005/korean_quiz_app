1. Particles (조사)

Particles are attached to nouns to show their function in a sentence.

Object Particle: 을/를

Marks the direct object of a sentence. This is the noun that the verb acts upon.

  • 를 is used after a noun ending in a vowel.
  • 을 is used after a noun ending in a consonant.

케이크를 만들었어요.

I made a cake. ('케이크' ends in a vowel, so we use '를'.)

Topic 은/는 vs. Subject 이/가

This is a key concept. Both can mark the subject, but with different nuances.

  • 이/가 (Subject Particle): Simply states a fact or identifies something new. Answers "Who/What did it?".
  • 은/는 (Topic Particle): Sets the topic of the sentence ("As for..."). Often used for comparison, contrast, or stating a general truth.

도서관이 조용해요.

(This is a simple fact) The library is quiet.

도서관은 조용해요.

(As for the library,) it's quiet. (This might imply something else, like the cafe, is noisy).

만 (Only)

Attached to a noun to mean "only" or "just" that noun.

커피만 마셔요.

I only drink coffee.

(이)나 (Or / Or something)

Has two main uses:

  • Choice from various options: Suggests one option when others are also possible. Often used when you're not picky. "How about...?"
  • Connecting two nouns: Means "or".

커피나 마실까요? (Choice)

Shall we drink coffee or something?

주말이나 금요일에 갈 거예요. (Connecting Nouns)

I will go on the weekend or Friday.

2. Verb Tenses & Forms (동사 시제와 형태)

V-는 것 (Turning a Verb into a Noun)

This grammar pattern changes a verb phrase into a noun phrase, similar to using "-ing" in English (e.g., "to take a picture" -> "taking a picture").

제 취미는 사진을 찍는 것이에요.

My hobby is the act of taking pictures.

V-고 있다 / N+중이다 (Present Progressive)

Both express that an action is currently in progress ("-ing"). -고 있다 is more common with general verbs, while 중이다 is often attached to nouns.

  • V-고 있다: `찾다 (to find) -> 찾고 있다 (is finding)`
  • N+중이다: `운전 (driving) -> 운전 중이다 (is in the middle of driving)`

지갑을 찾고 있어요.

I am looking for my wallet.

저는 지금 운전하는 중이에요.

I am in the middle of driving right now.

-아/어지다 (To Become / Get)

Attached to an adjective to show a change of state.

  • 춥다 (to be cold) + -어지다 -> 추워지다 (to become cold)
  • 따뜻하다 (to be warm) + -아지다 -> 따뜻해지다 (to become warm)

날씨가 추워졌어요.

The weather has become cold.

-(으)ㄹ게요 (I will... - As a promise)

Expresses the speaker's intention or a promise they are making to the listener. It has a softer feel than a simple future tense.

나중에 전화할게요.

I'll call you later. (A promise to the listener).

-(으)ㄹ지도 모르다 (Might) & -(으)ㄹ 것 같다 (Seems like)

Both are used to express possibility or speculation about the future.

비가 올지도 몰라요.

It might rain. (Literally: I don't know if it will rain).

비가 올 것 같아요.

It seems like it will rain.

3. Irregular Verbs (불규칙 동사)

'ㅅ' Irregular Verbs

The final consonant ㅅ of the verb stem disappears when it is followed by a vowel.

짓다 (to build) + -었어요 -> 짓 + 었어요 -> 지었어요

The 'ㅅ' is dropped before the vowel '어'.

'르' Irregular Verbs

When a verb stem ending in 르 is followed by -아/어, the 으 is dropped and an extra ㄹ is added to the syllable before it.

부르다 (to sing) + -었어요 -> 부 + ㄹ + 렀어요 -> 불렀어요

It becomes '불렀어요', not '부르었어요'.

4. Expressing Ability, Obligation & Decisions

못 V (Inability) vs. 안 V (Non-action)

못 expresses inability ("can't"), while 안 expresses a choice not to do something ("don't").

이해 못 해요.

I can't understand (due to lack of ability).

이해 안 해요.

I don't understand (by choice, I am not trying to).

-아/어야 하다 (Must / Have to)

Expresses obligation or necessity.

방을 청소해야 해요.

I have to clean my room.

-(으)ㄹ까 하다 (Thinking of doing...)

Expresses a vague intention or plan that is not yet finalized.

이번 여름에 바다에 갈까 해요.

I'm thinking of going to the sea this summer.

-기로 하다 (To decide to...)

Expresses a firm decision or resolution.

부산에 여행가기로 했어요.

We decided to travel to Busan.

-게 하다 (To make/let someone do...)

The causative form. It indicates that the subject causes someone else to perform an action.

기다리게 해서 죄송합니다.

I'm sorry for making you wait.

5. Numbers & Counters (숫자와 단위 명사)

When counting items, Koreans use Native Korean Numbers followed by a specific counter for the type of item.

Format: Noun + Native Number + Counter

Common Counters

  • 대: for vehicles, machines (cars, computers).
  • 자루: for long, thin objects (pencils, pens).
  • 장: for flat, thin objects (paper, tickets, photos).
  • 개: a general-purpose counter for things.

자동차 일곱 대 (Seven cars)

연필 아홉 자루 (Nine pencils)

종이 열 장 (Ten sheets of paper)

6. Locations & Directions (위치와 방향)

  • 앞: Front
  • 뒤: Back
  • 옆: Side / Next to
  • 사이: Between
  • 맞은편: Across from / Opposite side

은행은 학교 맞은편에 있어요.

The bank is across from the school.

7. Conjunctions & Reasons (연결과 이유)

V/A-거나 (Or)

Connects two or more verb or adjective clauses, meaning "or".

영화를 보거나 책을 읽을 거예요.

I will either watch a movie or read a book.

-(아/어)서 vs. -(으)니까 (Because)

Both express reason, but have different uses.

  • -(아/어)서: General reason. Commonly used to explain the reason for an emotion. Cannot be used with proposal (Let's...) or command (Please do...) sentences.
  • -(으)니까: The speaker's subjective reason. Can be used with proposals and commands.

너무 피곤해서 아무것도 할 수 없어요.

Because I'm so tired, I can't do anything.

시험에 합격해서 기뻐요.

I'm happy because you passed the exam. (Reason for emotion)

어제 일했으니까 피곤해요.

I worked yesterday, so I'm tired. (Both -해서 and -으니까 are okay here).

8. Expressing Surprise & Realization (놀람과 깨달음)

-네요 / -군요 / -는군요

These endings are used to express surprise, admiration, or a new realization upon directly seeing, hearing, or experiencing something.

  • -네요: The most common and versatile form.
  • -군요: Used with adjectives. Can sound slightly more formal or exclamatory than -네요.
  • -는군요: Used with action verbs. Same nuance as -군요.

와, 정말 맛있네요!

Wow, this is delicious! (Expressing surprise after tasting it).

아, 눈이 오네요! / 눈이 오는군요!

Oh, it's snowing! (Realization upon looking outside).

9. Comparisons & Superlatives (비교와 최상급)

A보다 B가 더... (B is more ... than A)

The particle 보다 attaches to the item being compared against. It means "than".

동생이 저보다 키가 커요.

My younger sibling is taller than me.

A만큼 (As ... as A) & A하고 같다 (Same as A)

Both are used to express equality.

저는 친구만큼 키가 커요.

I am as tall as my friend.

저는 친구하고 키가 같아요.

My height is the same as my friend.

제일 / 가장 (The most)

제일 and 가장 are interchangeable adverbs that mean "most" or "-est", forming the superlative.

이 중에서 뭐가 제일/가장 좋아요?

Among these, what is the best?

10. Politeness Levels & Requests (존댓말과 요청)

Polite Requests: -(으)세요 vs -아/어 주세요

Both are polite ways to ask someone to do something.

  • -(으)세요: General polite command/request.
  • -아/어 주세요: Implies the action is being done for the benefit of the speaker. It's often a slightly softer and more common request form.

여기에 이름을 쓰세요. (Write your name here.)

여기에 이름을 써 주세요. (Please write your name here [for me].)

Honorific Nouns & Verbs

Some words have a special, more polite (honorific) version used when referring to or speaking to someone of higher status.

  • 이름 (name) -> 성함 (honorific name)
  • 쓰다 (to write) -> 적다 (to write down, often used in forms)

여기에 성함을 적어 주세요.

Please write your name here. (A very polite and standard phrase on official documents).

11. Key Vocabulary & Phrases (주요 어휘 및 표현)

  • 영수증 (receipt) vs. 계산서 (bill). They are often used interchangeably, but `계산서` is what you ask for before paying, and `영수증` is what you get after.
  • 같다 (to be the same/similar) vs. 다르다 (to be different).
  • 덥다 (to be hot - weather) vs. 춥다 (to be cold - weather).
  • 켜다 (to turn on) vs. 끄다 (to turn off). (e.g., 불을 켜다/끄다 - turn the light on/off).
  • 길이 막히다: A set phrase meaning "the road is blocked" or "there is a traffic jam".
  • 살이 찌다 (to gain weight) vs. 살이 빠지다 (to lose weight).
  • 보다 / 만나다: Both can be used to mean "to meet". `보다` is slightly more casual ("to see each other").
  • 잘 모르겠어요: A very common and polite way to say "I'm not sure" or "I don't really know". It's softer than just saying `몰라요`.
