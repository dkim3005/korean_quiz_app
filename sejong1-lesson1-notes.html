Detailed Grammar Points

1. Topic Particle: Noun + 은/는

This is one of the most important particles in Korean. It attaches to a noun to show what the sentence is about. Think of it as saying "As for [noun]..." or "Speaking of [noun]...". It brings focus to the topic.

Rule:

  • After a noun ending in a **consonant** (받침 있음), use 은.
  • After a noun ending in a **vowel** (받침 없음), use 는.

Examples:

저는 학생입니다. (As for me, I am a student.) - '저' ends in a vowel.

선생님은 한국 사람입니다. (As for the teacher, he/she is Korean.) - '선생님' ends in a consonant.

이것은 책입니다. (As for this, it is a book.) - '이것' ends in a consonant.

2. "To be": Noun + 이에요/예요

This is the standard, polite way to say "am," "are," or "is." It's used to identify or describe a noun. This form is very common in everyday conversation.

Rule:

  • After a noun ending in a **consonant**, use 이에요.
  • After a noun ending in a **vowel**, use 예요.

Examples:

저는 미국 사람이에요. (I am an American.) - '사람' ends in a consonant.

저는 의사예요. (I am a doctor.) - '의사' ends in a vowel.

이름이 뭐예요? (What is your name?) - '뭐' ends in a vowel.

Tip: Question Form

To turn a statement into a question, you don't need to change the word order. Just raise the intonation at the end of the sentence.
Statement: 학생이에요. (I am a student.) → Question: 학생이에요? (Are you a student?)
